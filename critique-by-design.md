# Critique By Design: Redesigning Forbes 2025 World’s Highest Paid Athletes Data Table Visualization

## Step one: the visualization

_The visualization I chose was [Forbes 2025 World’s Highest Paid Athletes](https://www.forbes.com/lists/athletes/?sh=162054105b7e) that use a table as a visualization to depict their data on the 50 highest paid Athletes in the world, that include players in the sports categories of Auto Racing, Baseball, Basketball, Boxing, Football, Golf, and Soccer. The table organizes the players by Rank, their Name, Sport, Nationality, Total Earnings, On-field Earnings, and Off-field Earnings._ 

_Side note: Interestingly enough, the sport Tennis (and player Serena Williams) was included in the actual data set I downloaded, however it was not included in the original Forbes table. I will share my thoughts as to why this may be the case in a later section within this critique._

## Step two: the critique

_**Q1**: Usefulness.  Is it useful for the intended audience?  Does it communicate valuable information?  **Usefulness Score – 5**_

_**Q2**: Completeness.  Does the visualization have everything necessary to make it understandable? Completeness (of relevant data) **Score -4**_ 

_**Personal thoughts**: It would have been interesting to compare current data with historical data on the top-paid players in each sport category to show how earnings for top-earning athletes have increased over time. The article mentions this “trend” of significantly increased earnings for top-performing athletes, even as of 2023 metrics. The inclusion of additional historical comparison data would have better depicted the author’s story and had a larger impact. Also, “trend” is mentioned, yet the author chose a table instead of a trend line. The table is not terrible, but a trendline visualization would have been interesting too, and would have been even more impactful if there were historical data to compare it to._

_**Q3**: Perceptibility.  Can the reader understand the information with minimal effort? Is the visualization type appropriate?  Does it use illogical comparisons? 
**Perceptibility (Clear and Easy) Score - 7**_

_**Q4**: Truthfulness.  Is the visualization accurate, reliable and valid?  Is it representing what it says it is, and in the most complete and truthful manner? Does it misrepresent the data or make comparisons that aren't correct?
**Accurate and Valid Score - 9**_

_**Personal thoughts:** The author links another article describing the methodologies used to collect the data that offers transparency as to what specific earnings they included into the type of earning category and why. Additionally, they noted how they used income data that was earned from May 1st 2024 to May 2025, and how they had discussions with industry insiders and collaborated with and consulted with sports data firms (i.e. DODICI Sports, Formula Money, others), sports news reports, and salary databases (i.e. Capology, Spotrac, Over the Cap). [The Methodology Behind The World’s Highest-Paid Athletes For 2025](https://www.forbes.com/sites/brettknight/2025/05/15/the-methodology-behind-the-worlds-highest-paid-athletes-for-2025/)_

_**Q5:** Intuitiveness.  Is it easy to understand and clearly communicate the information?  If unfamiliar, does it include easy to understand instructions on how to interpret it?
**Intuitiveness (Familiar, easy to understand) Score - 8**_

_**Q6:** Aesthetics.  Is it interesting / enjoyable to look at?  Is it a good example of what a beautiful data visualization might look like?  Is it somewhere in the middle - pleasing but otherwise not distracting to look at? **Aesthetics Score - 3**_ 

_**Personal thoughts:** The table is straightforward but also boring. Additionally it lists 50 players across 6 different categories to be compared to. It feels like a less effective way to either scroll or look up a player of sport to make these comparisons._ 

_**Q7:** Engagement.  Does it lead the audience to learn more about the topic?  Does it inspire the audience to talk about the data or share it with others?
**Engagement to Data (draws audience in or out) Score - 6**_ 

_**Personal thoughts:** slightly? I foresee people simply scrolling through it and looking for players they recognize and maybe sharing those numbers with other people they know, but the “trends” described or even other less obvious trends cannot be identified solely from looking at the table. Specifically how most of the highest paid athletes are in basketball, which is noted in the article._


_**Q8:** Describe your overall observations about the data visualization here.  What stood out to you?  What did you find worked really well?  What didn't work well?_

_The title of this article stood out to me; the data visualization was not at the top of the article. You had to scroll to the bottom to find it, and you also had to scroll past some other linked Forbes articles, which I found odd and ineffective. Once I arrived at the table, it was not what I expected. It looked like an Excel spreadsheet, and I did not immediately notice that there were two search bars within the visualization to make it “easier” to navigate. It just felt like a lost opportunity to hook the audience, and it fell a bit short on creativity. What works well is that, in the end, it is a table, so it is simple and to the point. Also, I enjoyed the portraits of different athletes at the top of the article, but the art was not part of the actual data visualization._


_**Q9:** Who is the primary audience for this tool? Do you think this visualization is effective for reaching that audience?  Why or why not?_

_The target audience for this visualization/article is sports fans and, to some degree, the general public, since knowing how much top-earning athletes earn may draw attention. However, it is important to note that if the general public does click this article, they may find they do not recognize who all the athletes are, and because the table has too many comparisons, it will take too much effort to scroll through it and make associations with pay and the type of sport. It just takes too much work, and even if the person scrolling is a more “seasoned” sports and athlete fan._ 


_**Q10:** Based on your critique, what do you think you'll try to focus on in your redesign?   Any ideas or inspiration for how you can make a better data visualization?  What are you excited to try next?
Immediately, I knew I wanted to make this visualization more interesting and interactive by creating a stacked bar graph to not only show total earnings for each top-earning player, but also to display on- and off-field earnings in one place. To make it both visually more appealing and easier to register, however, this would mean 50 bars per player, so I am considering reducing the players depicted to the top 10-15. Just some initial thoughts. Additionally I want to see if I try to depict some of this data as a trend line since the author mentions “trend” throughout their article, so I will see how I can align that more with their words and communicate ideas, as noted in the Goodcharts book Chapter 4, that recommends we listen to how we describe our data to hint as to the type of visualization would make the best fit to the story we are hoping to tell._
